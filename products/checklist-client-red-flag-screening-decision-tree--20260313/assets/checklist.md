# Client Red Flag Screening Decision Tree for Freelancers

> Gives freelancers a visual yes/no decision tree to evaluate prospect calls and project briefs so they can confidently decline bad-fit clients before wasting proposal effort.

---

## Preparation

- [ ] **Research the prospect's company or personal brand before the call**
  *A legitimate client will have a traceable online presence. No website, sparse LinkedIn, or inconsistent branding signals instability or a non-serious buyer.*

- [ ] **Verify the prospect's identity and role using LinkedIn or a company directory**
  *Confirms you are speaking with a decision-maker who has authority to approve budgets and sign contracts, not someone who will loop in ten other approvers later.*

- [ ] **Review any project brief or intake form for vague or contradictory requirements**
  *Unclear briefs lead to scope creep and disputes. If they cannot articulate what they want in writing before the call, expect endless revision cycles.*

- [ ] **Search the client's name plus words like 'scam,' 'review,' or 'freelancer' online**
  *Previous freelancers often leave public warnings on forums like Reddit, Trustpilot, or Glassdoor about non-payment or abusive behavior.*

- [ ] **Check if the project timeline is realistic for the stated scope**
  *A client requesting a 60-page report in 48 hours either does not understand the work or plans to pressure you with artificial urgency throughout the engagement.*

## Execution

- [ ] **Ask the client directly why their previous freelancer or agency is no longer working with them**
  *The answer reveals how they treat contractors. Vague answers, blaming every past vendor, or admitting they 'just stopped responding' are major warning signs.*

- [ ] **Ask the client to describe their decision-making and approval process**
  *Multiple undisclosed stakeholders, committee approvals, or a boss who 'has final say' but never joins calls will stall projects and generate conflicting feedback.*

- [ ] **Directly ask for their confirmed budget range before presenting any numbers**
  *A client who refuses to share any budget range is either unserious, shopping for free consulting, or planning to lowball after you invest proposal time.*

- [ ] **Note whether the client interrupts, dismisses your questions, or talks over you**
  *Communication style on a sales call mirrors how they will behave as a client. Disrespect during the pitch phase intensifies once they have paid and feel entitled.*

- [ ] **Listen for the phrase 'this should be simple' or 'it won't take you long'**
  *This signals they plan to undervalue your expertise and will resist your quoted rate. It often precedes 'can you do it for less?' after you send the proposal.*

- [ ] **Ask whether they have a signed contract process or expect to work on a handshake**
  *Clients who resist written agreements have no accountability framework and create the conditions for scope disputes and non-payment.*

- [ ] **Confirm their payment terms and whether they accept milestone or upfront deposits**
  *A client who refuses any upfront payment or demands net-60 terms for a small project is transferring all financial risk onto you before work even begins.*

- [ ] **Ask how quickly they typically respond to emails and messages during a project**
  *Clients who go silent mid-project cause timeline delays for which you still get blamed. Their answer also reveals whether they will be an active, engaged collaborator.*

- [ ] **Ask what success looks like and how they will measure it at project close**
  *If they cannot define success criteria, there is no finish line. You will be stuck in perpetual revisions because 'done' will always feel subjective to them.*

## Review

- [ ] **Score the call immediately afterward using a 1–5 gut-feel rating before reviewing notes**
  *Your instinct captures nonverbal cues, tone, and energy that notes miss. A low gut score should trigger deeper scrutiny even if the call seemed fine on paper.*

- [ ] **Tally how many red flags triggered during the call using a simple count**
  *One red flag can be explained away. Three or more in a single screening session is a statistical pattern that predicts a difficult engagement, not an exception.*

- [ ] **Apply a personal red-flag threshold rule, such as 'three flags equals automatic decline'**
  *A pre-committed rule removes emotion from the rejection decision and prevents you from talking yourself into bad projects because you need the revenue.*

- [ ] **Draft and send a polite decline email within 24 hours if the threshold is met**
  *Delayed declines waste your calendar time on follow-up and give the prospect false hope, making the eventual rejection harder and more awkward for both parties.*

- [ ] **Log the client name, flags triggered, and outcome in a simple CRM or spreadsheet**
  *Freelance markets are small. Past red-flag clients sometimes return with new company names or refer others like them. A log protects you from repeat exposure.*

- [ ] **Reflect monthly on whether your screening questions caught the red flags that caused problems in active projects**
  *Your decision tree should evolve with experience. If a pain point surprised you, add a question that would have surfaced it during screening next time.*
