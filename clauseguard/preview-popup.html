This freelance agreement is heavily skewed in the client's favour. Immediate termination with no notice, a sweeping IP clause that captures pre-existing work, and a 5-year global non-compete are the most dangerous provisions. Negotiate these before signing.

Red Flags

🔴 Instant termination, zero notice
The client can end the contract without warning or reason, leaving you immediately unpaid for work in progress and with no time to line up new income.
🔴 IP clause seizes pre-existing work
Any prior code, designs, or assets you bring to the project become the client's property. You could lose tools and templates you rely on for other clients.
🟡 5-year global non-compete
This clause would restrict you from working for any competitor — worldwide — for 5 years after leaving. Likely unenforceable, but costly to challenge.

All Clauses

Termination Termination at will, no notice ▼
"Client may terminate this Agreement at any time, for any reason, with no notice required."
The client can terminate instantly without giving you time to wrap up, deliver, or invoice. You bear all the risk.
⚠ You could lose unpaid work and have zero runway to find your next client.
✦ Suggested change
Either party may terminate this Agreement with 14 days written notice. Upon termination, Client shall pay for all work completed up to the termination date.
Intellectual Property IP assignment including pre-existing work ▼
"All intellectual property created under this Agreement shall be owned exclusively by Client, including any pre-existing work incorporated into deliverables."
This transfers ownership of not only what you create for the client, but anything pre-existing that you incorporate — including your own libraries, frameworks, or templates.
⚠ You risk losing proprietary tools you've built over years and rely on for multiple clients.
✦ Suggested change
Client owns all deliverables created specifically for this project. Freelancer retains all rights to pre-existing tools, libraries, and methodologies, and grants Client a non-exclusive license to use them solely within the deliverables.
Non-Compete 5-year global non-compete ▼
"Freelancer shall not work for any competitor of Client for a period of 5 years following termination, globally."
A global 5-year non-compete is almost certainly unenforceable, but disputing it requires legal costs. Ask to remove it or narrow the scope significantly.
✦ Suggested change
Remove this clause entirely, or limit to: Freelancer shall not directly solicit Client's named customers for 6 months following termination.

Missing Protections

+ Late Payment Penalty
No clause covers what happens if the client pays late. Without it, you have no leverage and may wait months for payment.
Invoices unpaid after 14 days accrue interest at 1.5% per month. Client pays all reasonable collection costs.
+ Liability Cap
Without a liability cap, you could theoretically be sued for unlimited damages arising from your work.
Freelancer's total liability under this Agreement shall not exceed the fees paid in the 3 months preceding the claim.
+ Revision Scope
No limit on revisions means the client can request unlimited changes without additional payment.
Each project milestone includes up to 2 rounds of revisions. Additional revisions billed at the standard hourly rate.

Negotiation Tips

  1. Strike the global non-compete entirely — most courts won't enforce it anyway, and it signals good faith.
  2. Add a 14-day notice period before termination. Frame it as "enough time to hand over work cleanly."
  3. Carve out pre-existing IP explicitly — list your key tools by name in a schedule attached to the contract.
  4. Request a kill fee of 25–50% of remaining milestone value if the project is cancelled after work has begun.
