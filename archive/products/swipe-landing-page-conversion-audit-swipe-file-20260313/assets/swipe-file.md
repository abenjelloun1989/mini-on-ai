### 10. Trust Signal Hierarchy: Full-Page Layout Annotation

[ABOVE THE FOLD]
Headline + Subheadline + Primary CTA button
Media logos (earned press) — 3 to 5 maximum

[MID-PAGE — after scrolling]
Benefit bullets or feature breakdown
Client logo bar (recognizable brands only — 6 to 10 logos)

[JUST BELOW MIDDLE]
Long-form social proof: 2 to 3 testimonials with photo, full name, title, company
At least one testimonial should address a specific objection (e.g., ROI, time investment, ease of switching)

[ABOVE FINAL CTA]
Short-form trust indicators: security badge, privacy statement ('We never share your data'), review aggregate (e.g., '4.9/5 from 312 reviews on G2')
Final CTA with micro-copy ('Join 5,200+ marketers who've already downloaded this')

[FOOTER]
Privacy policy link, unsubscribe language, company address (especially critical for GDPR and improving email deliverability)

*Trust signals placed in the wrong order fail — this hierarchy maps to how attention naturally flows down the page. Media logos near the top establish authority before visitors know what you do. Client logos mid-page reinforce that 'people like me use this.' Testimonials mid-to-lower convert fence-sitters. Privacy language near the CTA directly reduces form abandonment. Audit any underperforming page against this hierarchy first.*

### 12. Headline Formula: Number + Adjective + Keyword + Outcome

FORMULA: [Specific Number] [Strong Adjective] [Core Keyword] to [Measurable Outcome] in [Timeframe]

EXAMPLES USING THE FORMULA:

1. '7 Underused Google Ads Tactics to Double Your ROAS in 60 Days'
2. '5 Counterintuitive Landing Page Fixes to Cut CPL by 30% Before Your Next Campaign'
3. '12 Proven Email Sequences to Revive Dead Leads Without Burning Your List'
4. '3 Simple Funnel Adjustments to Turn Browsers Into Booked Calls This Week'
5. '9 Critical Ad Copy Mistakes Costing You Qualified Leads (and How to Fix Each One)'

WHEN NOT TO USE THIS FORMULA:
- When your audience is senior/executive (can feel too listicle-y)
- When the service is deeply consultative and nuanced
- When you're targeting a sophisticated audience who's seen every '7 tactics' article ever written

*This headline formula is the workhorse of content-driven lead gen. The number creates pattern interrupt, the adjective filters for the right reader, the keyword confirms relevance, and the outcome + timeframe make the value proposition scannable and concrete. Use this for lead magnet download pages, webinar registrations, and free resource opt-ins targeting practitioners (managers, directors, specialists).*

### 14. Social Proof Testimonial Formats: Three Levels of Specificity

TIER 1 — Weak (Do Not Use):
'Great service, very helpful team. Would recommend!'
— John S., Marketing Manager

TIER 2 — Acceptable (Use If Nothing Better Available):
'Working with this team improved our lead quality significantly. Their process is thorough and they clearly know paid media.'
— Sarah K., VP Marketing, Fintech Startup

TIER 3 — High-Converting (Always Aim for This):
'Before we started, our CPL was $147 and less than 20% of leads were sales-qualified. After 90 days, we're at $68 CPL and 61% SQL rate. That change directly contributed to us hitting Q3 pipeline for the first time in two years. And they found the core issue in the first week — we'd been targeting the wrong job titles.'
— Darnell W., Director of Demand Generation, CloudPath (Series B, $40M raised)

FORMULA FOR SOLICITING TIER 3 TESTIMONIALS:
Ask clients: 'What was the specific problem before we worked together? What changed after, and can you put a number on it? What would you tell someone who was on the fence about working with us?'

*Specificity is the variable that separates testimonials that convert from testimonials that decorate. Before/after metrics, named objection resolution, and full identification (name, title, company, company descriptor) all add conversion weight. Coach your clients to give you Tier 3 testimonials by asking targeted questions — not 'can you leave us a review?' but the three-question framework above.*

### 16. CTA Button Copy Variations by Funnel Stage and Tone

TOP OF FUNNEL — Curiosity/Low Commitment:
→ 'Show Me the Breakdown'
→ 'Let Me See How It Works'
→ 'Yes, I Want the Free Checklist'
→ 'Send It to My Inbox'

MIDDLE OF FUNNEL — Value-Oriented:
→ 'Get My Custom Audit'
→ 'Find My Conversion Leaks'
→ 'Build My Free Strategy'
→ 'Unlock the Full Case Study'

BOTTOM OF FUNNEL — Action/Commitment:
→ 'Book My Strategy Call'
→ 'Claim My Free Audit Slot'
→ 'Start Fixing My Funnel Today'
→ 'Get Expert Eyes on My Account'

AVOID (Generic, Friction-Heavy):
✗ 'Submit'
✗ 'Click Here'
✗ 'Learn More'
✗ 'Sign Up'
✗ 'Register'

*CTA button copy is the most A/B tested element on landing pages for good reason — a single word change can move conversion rates 10–25%. First-person phrasing ('My' instead of 'Your') consistently outperforms in tests because it shifts psychological ownership to the visitor. Match CTA language to funnel temperature: cold traffic needs low-commitment language, warm retargeting traffic can handle stronger action language.*

WHEN TO BREAK THIS RULE:
- When you're intentionally friction-filtering for quality over quantity (high-ticket, limited capacity)
